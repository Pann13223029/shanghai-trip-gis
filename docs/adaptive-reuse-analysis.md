# Adaptive Reuse in Shanghai and Suzhou: A Comparative Analysis

## Introduction

Adaptive reuse — the practice of repurposing existing buildings or districts for new functions
while retaining their physical fabric — is one of the most consequential tools in sustainable
urban development (Yung & Chan, 2012). Rather than demolishing and rebuilding, adaptive reuse conserves the
embodied carbon locked in existing structures, preserves cultural memory embedded in built
form, and often maintains the spatial texture that makes urban places legible and liveable.

In rapidly modernising Chinese cities, the stakes are particularly high. Shanghai demolished
vast swathes of its lilong housing stock during the 1990s and 2000s — from over 9,200 sites in the 1950s to approximately 1,900 by 2014 (Zhong & Chen, 2017). The sites that survived — by design, accident, community resistance, or
commercial opportunism — offer a natural experiment in which strategies of preservation
actually work, and for whom.

This analysis compares six adaptive reuse sites drawn from the Shanghai / Suzhou trip GIS
dataset, spanning industrial conversion, neighbourhood transformation, institutional design,
and unbroken continuity of use across ten centuries.

## Analysis

### Four Models of Heritage Preservation

#### 1. Corporate-led: Xintiandi

Xintiandi is the most studied case of heritage preservation in contemporary China (Ren, 2008), and the
most instructive cautionary tale. Developer Shui On Land retained the shikumen stone-gate
facades of the 1920s residential blocks, creating a convincing architectural simulacrum of
the original neighbourhood. But the 2001-2005 redevelopment cleared all original residents
from the site. What remains is a heritage skin stretched over a premium retail and dining
programme targeted at middle-class consumers and foreign tourists — the spatial grammar of
a working-class lilong repurposed as an experience economy product.

The community_impact score in the dataset registers "mixed" rather than "negative," and that
nuance deserves attention. Xintiandi's commercial success generates significant tax revenue
that funds heritage protection elsewhere in the city. The Shikumen Open House Museum
(occupying one preserved interior) provides genuine public educational value. And the
preservation of fabric that might otherwise have been demolished entirely is not nothing.
The honest assessment is that Xintiandi optimises for architectural preservation and
commercial return at the direct expense of community continuity.

#### 2. Organic / Grassroots: Tianzifang and M50

Both Tianzifang and M50 began through informal occupation rather than master planning (Chen, 2015).
Artists and small studios moved into underutilised industrial and residential space ahead of
any official redevelopment mandate. This bottom-up genesis created something Xintiandi cannot
manufacture: genuine heterogeneity, the feeling of stumbling into a space still in process.

The trajectory of both sites, however, illustrates a structural problem with organic
preservation: it is temporally unstable. Success attracts commercial tenants who can pay
higher rents than the artists who created the appeal in the first place. M50's
sustainability notes document precisely this: "subsequent rent increases have displaced
smaller studios in favour of commercial galleries, replicating gentrification dynamics seen
at similar art districts globally." Tianzifang's notes record the same pattern, with the
qualification that some resident-artists still occupy upper floors — a partial mixed-use
model that delays but probably does not prevent full displacement.

The difference between Tianzifang (community_impact: mixed) and Xintiandi (community_impact:
mixed) is therefore a matter of phase and degree rather than kind. Tianzifang is further
back on the same trajectory. Both sites score heritage_value: 2, but Tianzifang's fabric
is genuinely residential in origin, while Xintiandi's is a reconstruction of the residential
aesthetic. The granularity of the scorecard does not capture this distinction.

#### 3. Government / Institutional: Power Station of Art and Suzhou Museum

These two sites represent state capacity deployed in the service of cultural infrastructure.
The Power Station of Art (PSA) is a direct conversion: the 35,000 m² 1985 Nanshi Power
Plant was adapted in 2012 without demolition, retaining the turbine hall, overhead cranes,
and the 165m chimney (repurposed as a public observation tower). Free admission and the
Shanghai Biennale mandate ensure broad public access. No displacement occurred because the
original use was industrial, not residential.

The Suzhou Museum is a more complicated case. Strictly speaking, it is not adaptive reuse
at all — it is new construction on a site adjacent to heritage. I.M. Pei's 2006 building
derives its legitimacy from proximity to the Humble Administrator's Garden and Lion Grove
Garden, and from design dialogue with Suzhou's whitewash-and-tile (粉墙黛瓦) vernacular.
Its heritage_value score of 3 reflects the significance of the institution and its
architectural achievement rather than the reuse of existing fabric. It belongs in this
comparison because it demonstrates an alternative institutional model: rather than inserting
a new programme into an old shell, new architecture can be designed to intensify the
heritage value of an existing cluster. The museum consolidates visitor traffic into a
walkable zone and provides interpretive infrastructure for the surrounding gardens.

Government-led preservation produces the most consistently positive community_impact scores
(positive for both PSA and Suzhou Museum) because it decouples preservation decisions from
the profit motive. The trade-off is that state cultural institutions are not neutral: the
PSA's curatorial programme is subject to the same constraints as any Chinese state cultural
body, and the choice of which industrial building to preserve — and which to demolish — is
never apolitical.

#### 4. Continuous Use: Longhua Temple

Longhua Temple resists the adaptive reuse frame entirely. The 977 AD pagoda has not been
"reused" for anything — it has been in continuous active use for over a thousand years,
with the monastic community maintaining the complex under its own stewardship. This gives
Longhua the dataset's highest heritage_value (3) and a community_impact of "positive,"
supported by sustainability notes that explicitly credit the active monastic community for
limiting tourist-driven commercialisation.

The lesson of Longhua is structural: a living institution with an ongoing internal purpose
is more durable than any preservation regime imposed from outside. The monastery does not
need a developer, a government programme, or a grassroots arts movement to maintain its
relevance. The challenge — managing tourist pressure on a functioning religious community —
is real but categorically different from the displacement dynamics that define every other
site in this comparison.

### Which Model Best Balances Preservation and Wellbeing?

There is no single answer. Government-led conversion (PSA) achieves the cleanest outcome
when the original use is non-residential: high public access, no displacement, embodied
carbon conserved. Continuous use (Longhua) achieves the most authentic outcome but requires
a pre-existing institution with deep internal purpose — it cannot be replicated by policy.
Organic/grassroots preservation (Tianzifang, M50) produces the richest social texture in
its early phase but is structurally vulnerable to the commercial success it generates.
Corporate-led preservation (Xintiandi) is the most scalable and commercially reliable
model, and the most socially costly.

The critical variable across all four models is whether the people who inhabited the space
before intervention are still there afterward. By that measure, Longhua and the PSA score
best; Xintiandi scores worst; and Tianzifang and M50 sit in a deteriorating middle ground.

## Spatial Observation

```

   South Bund

```

Four of the five Shanghai adaptive reuse sites in this analysis cluster within a roughly
3km radius centred on the former French Concession and adjacent Huangpu District. This is
not coincidental. The French Concession's colonial-era building stock was never as
systematically demolished as the lilong housing of other districts, because its architecture
signalled cultural capital to developers, planners, and residents alike. The Bund's 52
heritage buildings carry the same logic: foreign-designed structures on prime waterfront
land were preserved because their architectural pedigree made them legible as heritage even
to development interests that saw purely Chinese urban fabric as expendable.

The implication is that the geography of preservation in Shanghai reflects a hierarchy of
architectural legibility: buildings that read as "heritage" to international audiences — Art
Deco, colonial, industrial-modernist — were protected; the vernacular shikumen housing that
constituted the lived environment of most working-class Shanghainese was largely demolished.
Tianzifang and Xintiandi survived as exceptions, not as evidence of systematic policy. The
neighbourhoods that did not produce a Tianzifang or an M50 — which is most of them — were
simply cleared.

The Longhua Temple's location in southern Xuhui, outside the French Concession cluster,
underscores the point. Its survival owes nothing to architectural pedigree or commercial
potential; it survives because a functioning monastic institution with national-level
heritage status is difficult to displace even under development pressure.

## Portfolio Framing

This analysis demonstrates the ability to read spatial and qualitative GIS data critically —
extracting not just locational information but the preservation logic, community outcomes,
and structural trade-offs embedded in site records. The comparative framework connects
fieldwork-level observation to broader urban theory around gentrification, embodied carbon,
and the politics of heritage designation, showing how GIS data can support analytical
arguments as well as cartographic ones.
